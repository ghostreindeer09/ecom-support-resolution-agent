# Customer Communication Standards

**Document ID:** POL-COMM-016
**Effective Date:** January 1, 2026
**Last Updated:** March 15, 2026
**Applies To:** All customer-facing communications from support agents and automated systems

---

## Section 1: Communication Principles

All customer communications must adhere to the following principles:

1. **Empathy first**: Always acknowledge the customer's situation before providing information or a resolution
2. **Clarity**: Use plain language that any customer can understand. Avoid jargon, legal terminology, and internal process names
3. **Honesty**: Never mislead the customer about their options, timeline, or the likelihood of a favorable outcome
4. **Consistency**: Apply policies consistently. If a customer's request is denied by policy, do not imply that a different agent might give a different answer
5. **Privacy**: Never include sensitive information in customer communications — see PII protection guidelines below

---

## Section 2: Response Structure

Customer responses should follow this structure:

1. **Acknowledgment** (1 sentence): Show you understand what happened and how the customer feels
   - Good: "I'm sorry to hear your item arrived damaged — I understand how frustrating that must be."
   - Bad: "Hello, thank you for contacting support."

2. **Decision** (1–2 sentences): Clearly state what action you are taking or can take
   - Good: "Based on our policy, we can offer you a full refund for the damaged item."
   - Bad: "Per section 3.2 of policy document POL-RR-001, your claim has been adjudicated."

3. **Explanation** (1–3 sentences): Explain why, in plain terms, without citing internal documents
   - Good: "Since the item arrived damaged, our policy covers a full refund and we'll send you a prepaid return label."
   - Bad: "As per our returns and refunds policy, damaged items received within the 7-day damage claim window are eligible for full refund processing as outlined in section 3, paragraph 2."

4. **Next steps** (1–2 sentences): Tell the customer exactly what happens next
   - Good: "You'll see the refund in your account within 5–10 business days. I've also emailed you a return label."
   - Bad: "Your case has been processed."

5. **Closing** (1 sentence): Offer reassurance or additional help
   - Good: "Please don't hesitate to reach out if you need anything else."

---

## Section 3: PII Protection in Communications

Customer-facing communications must NEVER contain the following personally identifiable information:

1. **Order IDs or tracking numbers**: Use generic references ("your recent order," "the order you contacted us about") instead of specific IDs
2. **Email addresses**: Do not include the customer's email address in the response body
3. **Payment method details**: Never reference card numbers, even partially (no "your card ending in 1234")
4. **Full names**: Do not use the customer's full name (first + last) in the response body — first name only is acceptable
5. **Physical addresses**: Do not repeat the customer's full address in the response body
6. **Phone numbers**: Do not include phone numbers in the response body

If the response requires referencing any of these identifiers, use generic descriptions:
- "Your recently delivered order" instead of "Order #12345"
- "Your payment method on file" instead of "Your Visa ending in 4567"
- "Your delivery address" instead of "123 Main St, Anytown, CA 90210"

---

## Section 4: Language Restrictions

The following phrases and language patterns must be avoided in all customer communications:

**Absolute promises:**
- "We guarantee..." — unless citing a specific, documented guarantee
- "We promise..." — promises create liability
- "We always..." or "We never..." — absolute statements can be disproven

**Legal conclusions:**
- "You are legally entitled to..." — legal determinations should not be made by support agents
- "The law requires us to..." — refer to legal team if law is relevant
- "We are obligated to..." — unless citing a clearly documented obligation

**Accusatory language:**
- "You didn't..." or "You failed to..." — rephrase neutrally
- "That's not what happened" — do not contradict the customer's account directly
- "You should have..." — avoid blame

**Dismissive language:**
- "Unfortunately, there's nothing we can do" — always offer an alternative path, even if it's escalation
- "That's our policy" — explain the reason behind the policy
- "You agreed to the terms when you purchased" — never reference terms acceptance as justification

---

## Section 5: Escalation Communication

When a case is being escalated:

1. Use the standard holding message template: "Thank you for your patience. I'm referring your case to our specialist team to ensure we handle this correctly. You'll hear back within [timeline]."
2. Never tell the customer why their case is being escalated internally (e.g., "I'm flagging this for fraud review")
3. Always provide a timeline for the specialist team's response
4. If the case is urgent (fraud, legal, safety), do not promise a specific outcome — only confirm that it will be reviewed quickly

---

## Section 6: Tone Calibration

Adjust communication tone based on the situation:

**For positive resolutions (approvals, refunds):**
- Warm, efficient, and reassuring
- "Great news — we're happy to process a full refund for you."

**For negative resolutions (denials):**
- Empathetic, clear, and solution-oriented
- "I understand this isn't the answer you were hoping for, and I'm sorry about that. Here's why..."
- Always end with an alternative or next step, even if it's "contact us again if your situation changes"

**For uncertain outcomes (escalations, investigations):**
- Professional, transparent about uncertainty, with clear next steps
- "I want to make sure we handle this thoroughly. I'm bringing in our specialist team for review."

**For fraud-related cases:**
- Neutral, non-accusatory, no indication that fraud is suspected
- "Your case requires additional review by our specialist team to ensure we get the best outcome for you."
